Design of CALL Applications

Designing effective CALL

Published on March 10, 2025

The design of CALL applications goes beyond simply integrating technology into the language learning experience. Effective CALL design requires a careful balance between different aspects such as pedagogy, learners' needs, and technological affordances. As digital tools become more sophisticated, the challenge is not just about selecting the right software but also structuring CALL designs to various learning environments.

In her article, Sun (2017) introduces 'design for learning', a CALL design approach that shifts the focus to the learning process. She references two design approaches to support her argument: Colpaert (2006) pedagogy-driven CALL design, which emphasizes the specification of what is needed for language teaching and learning in a specific context, and Social CALL, which positions learners as active agents in the target language and emphasizes learner agency and authentic interaction. Effective CALL design requires understanding students’ linguistic, cultural, and technological needs and background. She advocates for a dynamic and forward-oriented method structured in four phases:

  • Design for configuration by anticipating learners’ needs and allowing adjustments,
  • Design for orchestration by supporting teachers in managing various learning processes
  • Design for reflection by incorporating systems evaluation mechanisms to assess effectiveness, and
  • Design for re-design ensuring flexibility for continuous improvements. CALL design.

CALL design should position learners as active participants, fostering interaction with people and resources. Additionally, it should also consider the architecture of learning which includes different factors such as digital tools, materials, physical spaces, and social roles.

In a recent study on designing smartphone language learning apps for older learners (+60), Puebla et al. (2022) adopted a user-centered design (UCD) approach known as design thinking (DT). By involving target learners throughout the development process and incorporating their specific needs, preferences, and abilities, the researchers gained a deeper insight into older learners’ requirements and proposed a set of principles to guide future technology solutions. They followed five steps -empathize, define, ideate, prototype, and test – grouped into three main phases. In the phase I ‘Needfinding’, interviews highlighted older learners’ needs for social interaction, active lifestyle, and authentic communication. During the phase II ‘Brainstorming’, canvas was used as idea generation and lead to the idea of a platform matching older L2 learners based on their profiles, target languages, and common interests. In the phase III ‘Prototype’, participants tested the app, completed tasks, and answererd a feedback questionnaire. The app Sprachen Tanz foster both social and collaborative interaction by creating a learning community where knowledge is constructed together, and also learner autonomy by allowing users to choose roles (tutor or learner), preferred activities, and learning formats (small group or partners). Although the results show significant potential for promoting language acquisition among older users, the small and homogeneous sample restricts the generalizability of these findings.

In another recent investigation of adults’ online language education, Bergdahl (2022) highlights how teachers’ learning designs can drive engagement and language development in L2 learners. First, she advises that teachers should begin by assessing their learners’ linguistic and digital needs alongside the available technological tools. Next, educators are encouraged to identify core elements that can promote engagement in online language acquisition such as scaffolding tasks, balancing real-time and self-paced activities, and integrating different skills within the same session. Once these elements are in place, she suggests organizing them in a coherent learning sequence and gathering feedback through classroom observations. Finally, teachers refine their learning designs ensuring that the CALL application remain adaptive and relies on both teachers’ digital competences and context-aware strategies that encourage active participation.
